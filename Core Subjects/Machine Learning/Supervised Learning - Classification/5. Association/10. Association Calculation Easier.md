The Apriori algorithm works in iterative **phases** to separate "frequent" itemsets from "infrequent" ones.

The "separation" logic relies on the **Apriori Principle** (or Downward Closure Property):

> _If an itemset is infrequent, all of its supersets must also be infrequent._

This principle allows the algorithm to prune (separate/discard) huge chunks of the search space without having to check every single possibility.

Here are the phases of the algorithm, typically described as the **Join** and **Prune** steps, which repeat as $k$ (the size of the itemset) increases.

### Phase 1: Initialization (k=1)

The algorithm starts by scanning the entire dataset to count the frequency of every single individual item.

- **Action:** Calculate **Support** for each item.
    
- **Separation:** Any item that does not meet the **Minimum Support Threshold** (e.g., 50%) is discarded immediately.
    
- **Result:** You are left with $L_1$ (Frequent 1-itemsets).
    

### Phase 2: The Iterative Loop (k = 2, 3, 4...)

This is where the magic happens. The algorithm repeats these two sub-phases until no more frequent itemsets can be found.

#### A. The Join Step (Candidate Generation)

It creates new "Candidate" itemsets ($C_k$) by joining the frequent itemsets found in the previous step ($L_{k-1}$) with themselves.

- _Example:_ If {Milk} and {Bread} were frequent in step 1, it joins them to create a candidate: {Milk, Bread}.
    

#### B. The Prune Step (The Separation)

This is where the filtering happens. It validates the candidates created in the Join step.

1. **subset Pruning:** Before even looking at the database, it checks if any _subset_ of the new candidate was previously known to be infrequent. If so, kill the candidate immediately. (This saves processing time).
    
2. **Support Counting:** Scan the database to count how often these remaining candidates actually appear.
    
3. **Threshold Separation:** If a candidate's count < Minimum Support, it is **separated** (discarded). If count $\ge$ Minimum Support, it is kept.
    

### Phase 3: Rule Generation

Once the algorithm stops (because no candidates survived the Prune step), it takes all the "survivor" itemsets and generates rules.

- It calculates **Confidence** for every possible rule from the itemsets.
    
- **Separation:** Any rule with Confidence < Minimum Confidence is discarded.

### A Concrete Example Trace

Imagine `Min_Support = 2` (Item must appear 2 times to be "Frequent").

**Database:**

- T1: {A, B, D}
    
- T2: {B, C, D}
    
- T3: {A, B}
    
- T4: {A, D}
    

**Iteration 1 (k=1):**

- **Count:** A=3, B=3, C=1, D=3.
    
- **Separation:** C appears only once ($1 < 2$). **C is pruned.**
    
- **Survivors ($L_1$):** {A}, {B}, {D}
    

**Iteration 2 (k=2):**

- **Join:** Combine survivors {A}, {B}, {D} to make pairs: {A,B}, {A,D}, {B,D}.
    
- **Count:** Scan database for these pairs.
    
    - {A,B}: 2 times (Keep)
        
    - {A,D}: 2 times (Keep)
        
    - {B,D}: 2 times (Keep)
        
- **Separation:** All survived.
    
- **Survivors ($L_2$):** {A,B}, {A,D}, {B,D}
    

**Iteration 3 (k=3):**

- **Join:** Combine pairs to make triplets: {A, B, D}.
    
- **Prune (Subset Check):** Are all subsets of {A, B, D} frequent?
    
    - {A,B} is frequent? Yes.
        
    - {A,D} is frequent? Yes.
        
    - {B,D} is frequent? Yes.
        
    - _Result:_ {A, B, D} is a valid candidate.
        
- **Count:** Scan database. {A, B, D} appears in T1 only (Count = 1).
    
- **Separation:** $1 < 2$. **{A, B, D} is pruned.**
    

**Stop:** No candidates left.


We stopped at **Phase 3 (finding Frequent Itemsets)**. Our survivors were the pairs: **{A, B}, {A, D}, and {B, D}**.

Now we move to **Phase 4: Generating and Filtering Rules**.

---

### Phase 4: Rule Generation & Calculation

We don't just want to know that "A and B go together." We want to know the **direction** of the relationship. Does buying A _cause_ the purchase of B?

From the itemset **{A, B}**, we can generate two possible rules:

1. **Rule 1:** $A \rightarrow B$ ("If you buy A, you also buy B")
    
2. **Rule 2:** $B \rightarrow A$ ("If you buy B, you also buy A")
    

To decide which rule is valid, we calculate **Confidence**.

#### 1. Calculating Confidence

Formula:

$$\text{Confidence}(X \rightarrow Y) = \frac{\text{Support}(X \text{ and } Y)}{\text{Support}(X)}$$

(Think of it as: Out of all the times X appeared, how many times did Y also appear?)

**Using our Data:**

- Total transactions involving A: **3** (T1, T3, T4)
    
- Total transactions involving B: **3** (T1, T2, T3)
    
- Total transactions with BOTH A and B: **2** (T1, T3)
    

Let's test Rule 1 ($A \rightarrow B$):

$$\text{Confidence} = \frac{\text{Count}(A, B)}{\text{Count}(A)} = \frac{2}{3} \approx \mathbf{66.7\%}$$

Let's test Rule 2 ($B \rightarrow A$):

$$\text{Confidence} = \frac{\text{Count}(A, B)}{\text{Count}(B)} = \frac{2}{3} \approx \mathbf{66.7\%}$$

The Separation (Pruning):

If your Minimum Confidence Threshold was set to 70%, both of these rules would be rejected (pruned). Even though A and B appear together often, the relationship isn't strong enough for your strict standard.

---

### Phase 5: The "Lift" Check (The Final Trap)

There is one major flaw in Confidence: Popularity Bias.

If item B is incredibly popular (like "Milk"), almost everyone buys it. A rule like "If you buy A $\rightarrow$ you buy Milk" might have high confidence just because everyone buys Milk, not because A has any relationship to it.

To fix this, we calculate **Lift**. This is the most crucial metric for "Is this model actually useful?"

Formula:

$$\text{Lift}(A \rightarrow B) = \frac{\text{Confidence}(A \rightarrow B)}{\text{Support}(B)}$$

- **Lift = 1:** The two items are independent. (Coincidence).
    
- **Lift > 1:** There is a strong relationship. (Useful rule).
    
- **Lift < 1:** They are negatively correlated. (Buying A means you likely _won't_ buy B).
    

**Calculating Lift for $A \rightarrow B$:**

- Confidence ($A \rightarrow B$) = $0.67$
    
- Support (B) = Count(B) / Total Transactions = $3 / 4 = 0.75$
    

$$\text{Lift} = \frac{0.67}{0.75} = \mathbf{0.89}$$

Conclusion:

Since Lift (0.89) is less than 1, this relationship is actually weaker than chance. Buying A actually makes you slightly less likely to buy B than a random stranger.

This illustrates why we need all three metrics:

1. **Support:** Filters out rare items.
    
2. **Confidence:** Checks the reliability of the direction.
    
3. **Lift:** Checks if the relationship is real or just popularity.

from mlxtend.frequent_patterns import association_rules
